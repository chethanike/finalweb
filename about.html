Literature Survey

Side-channel attacks (SCAs) present a formidable threat to cryptographic implementations, exploiting physical leakages rather than algorithmic flaws. Among various SCA techniques, power analysis stands out for its simplicity and effectiveness. Techniques like Simple Power Analysis (SPA) and Differential Power Analysis (DPA), introduced by Kocher et al. in 1999, laid the groundwork for modern SCAs.

While SPA visually interprets patterns in power traces, DPA uses statistical correlation across multiple traces to extract key bits, showing resilience against noise. The evolution of DPA has led to advanced forms like higher-order DPA and Correlation Power Analysis (CPA), the latter introduced by Brier et al. in 2004. CPA improves attack accuracy by correlating predicted intermediate values, modeled using Hamming weight or distance, with actual power traces.

Over time, the scope of SCAs has expanded beyond smart cards to include microcontrollers, FPGAs, and general-purpose CPUs. Affordable platforms such as Arduino, ESP32, and ChipWhisperer have democratized SCA research, offering educational and experimental opportunities. Symmetric algorithms like DES and AES, and asymmetric ones like RSA, have all shown vulnerabilities to SCAs. DES, in particular, has been a focus due to its structured 16-round Feistel design, which leaks data-dependent power consumptionâ€”especially in its S-box and key scheduling operations.

Preprocessing steps like trace alignment, noise filtering, and dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA) are critical for improving attack success rates on noisy, software-based systems. To combat SCAs, various countermeasures have emerged, such as masking, hiding, and randomization. Masking injects randomness to obscure key-dependent computations but is vulnerable to higher-order attacks. Hiding strategies, like inserting dummy instructions or balancing current draw, reduce distinguishable power patterns, while randomization shuffles execution sequences or injects noise to reduce trace predictability. These solutions, however, often involve trade-offs in performance, complexity, or compatibility.

Machine learning (ML) has further advanced SCA methodologies. ML techniquesâ€”ranging from SVMs and decision trees to deep learning models like convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent networks (e.g., LSTM)â€”have demonstrated remarkable ability in key recovery tasks, especially under noisy or misaligned conditions. CNNs excel in analyzing power traces (spatial data), whereas LSTMs are effective on timing information (temporal data). While tools like ASCAD have benchmarked ML-based attacks primarily on AES, less attention has been given to DES or to timing-based attacks in embedded systems.

Timing SCAs, though often underestimated, are particularly threatening due to their non-invasive nature. First introduced by Kocher, timing attacks exploit execution time variations, such as those introduced by conditional branching or data-dependent memory access patterns. DES, although deterministic, can still exhibit minute timing variations based on Hamming distances and key dependencies. Boneh and Brumley later demonstrated remote timing attacks across networks, further highlighting the real-world feasibility of these threats. However, practical demonstrations of timing SCAs on affordable platforms like Raspberry Pi remain sparse.

Timestamp-based key generation represents another often-overlooked vulnerability. Systems that rely on predictable timestamps for entropy can be trivially brute-forced. While public-key systems have been shown to suffer from weak entropy sources, similar studies for symmetric algorithms like DES are lacking. Raspberry Pi, with its accessibility and realistic computing environment, serves as an ideal platform for exploring such vulnerabilities.

Electromagnetic (EM) SCAs are similarly concerning due to their non-contact nature. Cryptographic operations generate EM emissions that can be captured and analyzed using tools like CPA or DPA. Microcontrollers and embedded systems are particularly vulnerable due to limited hardware protections. Although ML-based EM analysis has gained traction, it is often limited to costly lab equipment, which hinders adoption in educational or low-resource settings.

This thesis addresses these gaps through a unified, lightweight countermeasure framework focused on DES. It introduces a three-pronged masking strategy: dynamic XOR masking, tokenized indexing, and format-preserving scrambling. These methods disrupt side-channel leakage at the round level, ensuring compatibility with both DES and 3DES implementations. Importantly, the countermeasures maintain ciphertext format and support existing decryption environments without architectural changes.

The work also incorporates practical SCA analysis using ML. One component demonstrates real-time DES key prediction from timing data using LSTM networks deployed on a Raspberry Pi. Another builds a portable EM side-channel detection toolkit based on ML models, offering a cost-effective, hands-on educational platform.

In conclusion, this research bridges the gap between theoretical cryptographic strength and real-world physical vulnerability. It provides a practical upgrade path for securing legacy DES-based systems, while also laying the foundation for adapting similar protections to modern block ciphers like AES.

Research Gap

Despite substantial progress in side-channel analysis (SCA), critical gaps persist particularly in practical, reproducible, and cost-effective studies focused on legacy encryption algorithms like DES. The vast majority of existing research concentrates on AES and depends heavily on high-end laboratory equipment, which limits accessibility and scalability.

DES remains under-investigated, especially on widely available platforms like the Raspberry Pi. Timing attacks, while theoretically straightforward, have yet to see robust real-world implementations leveraging modern machine learning (ML) techniques on resource-constrained devices. Power and electromagnetic (EM) side-channel attacks, though potent, are often constrained by the high cost of instrumentation and the level of technical expertise required.

Moreover, there is a striking lack of comprehensive studies that explore multi-leakage vectorsâ€”such as the combined analysis of timing, power, and EM signals. Current countermeasures primarily target AES and overlook vulnerabilities specific to DES operating modes like ECB and CBC.

This creates an urgent need for a unified, accessible, and scalable platform capable of supporting DES-focused SCA research and countermeasure development. Such a platform should integrate ML-based defenses and operate effectively on low-cost hardware, closing the gap between theoretical vulnerability and practical security.

Research Problem

This study focuses on improving the resilience of the Data Encryption Standard (DES) against physical side-channel attacks (SCAs) in resource-limited and legacy environments. Despite its theoretical security, DES implementations are vulnerable to attacks exploiting unintended leakages like power consumption and electromagnetic emissions, especially in embedded systems and IoT devices that often lack adequate protections. To address this, the study proposes a novel three-layer masking method combining Dynamic XOR Masking, Tokenized Indexing, and Format-Preserving Scrambling. These techniques introduce dynamic, input-dependent masks, randomize memory access patterns, and maintain data format integrity, respectively. This modular, mode-independent approach allows seamless integration with existing DES operations in ECB and CBC modes without altering external behavior or requiring system overhauls.

The research further aims to theoretically and empirically validate the effectiveness of the masking framework. It demonstrates how the masking increases entropy in key-dependent intermediate values, reducing the effectiveness of attacks such as Differential Power Analysis (DPA) and Correlation Power Analysis (CPA), while maintaining minimal computational and memory overhead. Empirical validation involves simulating power and timing leakages, and applying various SCA methods to both masked and unmasked DES, evaluating security improvements using metrics like signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and mutual information analysis (MIA). Finally, the study explores the feasibility of deploying the masking scheme in existing constrained environments like embedded systems and legacy hardware, ensuring compatibility and practical applicability without disrupting current cryptosystems. This work bridges theoretical cryptographic security and practical implementation, providing a viable solution to protect legacy encryption against modern side-channel threats.

Methodology

Power-Based Side-Channel Attack Analysis

This project outlines the development of a power-based side-channel attack system targeting the DES encryption algorithm. The system includes embedded hardware for real-time power measurement, data preprocessing, and machine learning-based key inference. The core goal was to capture power traces during encryption and use these to predict plaintext and encryption keys.

Initial implementation used the Arduino Nano and INA219 power sensor to log voltage, current, power, and energy during encryption. Due to memory and processing constraints, the system was upgraded to the ESP32 microcontroller, which allowed for real-time display, Bluetooth communication, and extended processing capability.

The final system included a microSD module for logging, a 16x2 LCD for displaying results, and Bluetooth commands for control. The ESP32 periodically polled the INA219 sensor and logged cumulative energy data.

The system supported command-based control through Bluetooth:

  • STARTLOG: Starts logging and resets counters.
  • STOPLOG: Stops logging and flushes data.
  • GETLOG: Sends logged data to the user.

Python was used for preprocessing the raw sensor data. For each encryption event, 16 statistical features were extracted (mean, max, min, and standard deviation for voltage, current, power, and energy). Features were normalized and encoded to prepare them for machine learning.

An MLP neural network with three hidden layers (128, 64, 32 neurons) was built using TensorFlow. Trained for 1000 epochs with batch size 16, the model achieved 84% accuracy in plaintext prediction. The model and its encoders/scalers were serialized for deployment on embedded hardware for live prediction.

Real-world encryption sessions were executed to test the full system. The ESP32 processed power data in real-time, and the machine learning model predicted plaintext and key values, which were displayed on an LCD. Bluetooth enabled remote control and validation of the systemâ€™s functionality.

The system demonstrated a complete low-power, low-cost, and effective methodology for power-based side-channel cryptanalysis using embedded hardware and AI. It is modular and can be adapted for other algorithms, such as AES, or include countermeasures.



Time-Based Side-Channel Attack Analysis

This section introduces two timing-based strategies to infer DES encryption keys: timestamp-based key generation and deep learning using LSTM to exploit timing variations in encryption latency.

The system simulates scenarios where DES keys are generated using Unix timestamps. Keys are generated for a time window and compared to a target key using byte-level and bit-level similarity. The most similar keys are ranked and reported, showcasing the vulnerability of deterministic key generation from time-based entropy sources.

An LSTM network was trained to predict 64-bit DES keys based on encryption latency and side-channel features such as Hamming weights and distances. These features were normalized and input as sequences suitable for LSTM processing. The model used sigmoid-activated outputs and binary crossentropy loss to predict each key bit independently.

The LSTM model was tested with various combinations of features, confirming that prediction accuracy improved with the addition of timing and statistical features. Final output was compared visually with actual keys to evaluate prediction accuracy.

The combined approaches demonstrate the risks of timing leakage in cryptographic systems. Timestamp analysis reveals predictable key generation vulnerabilities, while LSTM modeling shows how timing variations can statistically reveal key structure. Both methods emphasize the importance of securing implementations against side-channel threats.



EM Analysis Methodology

The primary objective of this research was to develop a portable hardware-based system capable of detecting side-channel vulnerabilities through the analysis of electromagnetic (EM) traces using machine learning. The following methodology outlines the critical stages in achieving this from dataset preparation to final hardware deployment and evaluation.

At this stage of the research, the aim was to develop a quality dataset that would be used to train a machine learning model to detect electromagnetic (EM) side-channel vulnerabilities. Due to time and equipment limitations, new EM traces could not be obtained in a laboratory setting; therefore, the research team utilized pre-existing validated datasets containing EM signals generated during encryption processes with the DES algorithm.

All traces captured the unique EM activity emitted by hardware during encryption using a 16-digit plaintext and a 16-digit key. These traces served as the input data for the machine learning model. Each trace was labeled as either "Vulnerable" or "Non-Vulnerable" using a threshold derived from the mean value of the trace. Traces with mean values above the threshold were labeled as vulnerable, and those below as non-vulnerable.

This minimalistic labeling approach reduced computational complexity while still providing enough information for the machine learning model to learn useful classification patterns. Before feeding the EM traces to the model, preprocessing was performed to improve model efficiency and adapt to limited hardware support.

Normalization was used to standardize EM signal values, typically to a zero mean and one standard deviation. This helped reduce bias from differences in magnitudes and ensured consistency across traces. Feature extraction followed, using statistical featuresâ€”mean, standard deviation, and varianceâ€”to summarize trace behavior with minimal computational cost.

This approach balanced simplicity and effectiveness, enabling real-time analysis on resource-constrained platforms like the Raspberry Pi. The lightweight feature set supported low-resource deployment without sacrificing classification accuracy.

Four machine learning models were evaluated for classifying EM traces: Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine (SVM), Decision Tree, and Random Forest. These models offered different trade-offs between accuracy, complexity, and computational requirements.

Each model was trained using the preprocessed statistical features. Performance was evaluated using accuracy, precision, and recall. Random Forest consistently achieved the highest accuracy, thanks to its ensemble of decision trees which reduced overfitting and improved pattern detection.

Due to its performance and reasonable resource requirements, Random Forest was selected for deployment. It struck the right balance between accuracy and feasibility for real-time embedded implementation.

The trained Random Forest model was deployed on a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B, chosen for its affordability, Python support, and GPIO capabilities. Raspberry Pi OS (64-bit) was used for compatibility and performance. A headless setup enabled remote installation via SSH.

Raspberry Pi Connect was used for GUI and terminal access via Wi-Fi, allowing remote control and testing. Necessary Python librariesâ€”NumPy, pandas, scikit-learn, and GPIO librariesâ€”were installed to support model inference and hardware communication.

A 16x2 LCD display was connected to the Raspberry Pi using GPIO. Python code was developed to display classification results, offering portable, real-time output without needing external monitors.

The process involved remotely inputting a 16-digit key and plaintext combination. Rather than encrypting in real time, the system fetched a matching EM trace from the dataset. This trace was fed into the trained classifier, which determined and displayed the vulnerability status on the LCD.

The final system was portable, low-cost, and independent of lab-grade hardware, making it ideal for educational and small-scale research environmentsâ€”fulfilling the project's goals of accessibility and simplicity.



Masking Technique

This paper presents a universal countermeasure framework to enhance the resistance of DES implementations against side-channel attacks such as Differential Power Analysis (DPA) and cache-timing attacks. The methodology consists of two main components: Framework Design and Masking Integration and Evaluation.

The approach involves a hybrid masking technique using three complementary countermeasures that provide layered defense across different stages of the DES encryption process while maintaining compatibility and performance.

The first technique introduces randomness at the input stage by applying an XOR operation between the plaintext and a pseudo-randomly generated mask. This dynamic masking alters the intermediate states during encryption, preventing deterministic correlations between inputs and side-channel emissions, thus mitigating first-order DPA attacks.

The second technique applies Tokenized Indexing to the ciphertext. Tokens are generated based on DES S-box dynamics, and the ciphertext blocks are reordered pseudo-randomly. This disrupts predictable memory access patterns, strengthening resistance to cache-timing and profiling attacks.

The third countermeasure involves format-preserving scrambling of the permuted ciphertext using a Feistel-based transformation. This reversible scrambling distributes remaining patterns in the ciphertext, enhancing protection against chosen-ciphertext and pattern-matching attacks.

Together, these three techniques form a multi-layered defense mechanism that protects against a wide range of SCAs including power, EM, and cache-based timing attacks. The framework is adaptable to different stages of encryption for comprehensive protection.

The integrated masking techniques were embedded into the DES algorithm at strategic pointsâ€”XOR masking before encryption, Tokenized Indexing after encryption, and format-preserving scrambling as the final step. These modifications introduced unpredictability at every stage while preserving encryption functionality.

The framework was tested on three platforms: Arduino Uno (low-power microcontroller), Raspberry Pi 4 (embedded system), and Xilinx Spartan-6 (FPGA). These platforms allowed for varied attack models and side-channel measurements including EM, power, and timing data.

Machine learning models, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), were used to analyze the captured side-channel data and evaluate the frameworks ability to suppress leakages. Performance was measured in terms of DPA resistance, cache-timing attack prevention, and chosen-ciphertext protection.

A comparative study was conducted to evaluate DES implementations with and without the proposed masking techniques. The results demonstrated significant improvements in side-channel resistance with minimal performance overhead, validating the practicality and efficiency of the framework.

The balance between added security and low computational cost ensures the framework is lightweight and suitable for real-world cryptographic hardware applications, delivering on its goal of robust, accessible, and efficient side-channel protection.
